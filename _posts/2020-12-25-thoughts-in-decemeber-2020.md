**thought #1**

As I'm exploring more fields of computer science, not necessarily through taking classes in them, but by being in the field and receiving exposure to different information and people, I find myself returning to what I originally wished to do as a kid.

From memory, I was in Beijing, on break from elementary school, so I came to grandma's house. Sitting on the bamboo sofa cover, a chunky, grey television could only display a handful of channels. Either Discovery or National Geographic was playing, and I remember being mesmorized by their theme of a fully automated transportation and road system. Autonomous cars were integrated with the roads and public transportation system. Maybe it was my parents in the car industry or my fascination for fantasical stories, but even though they only laid out a blueprint for it, the possibilities of sounded so cool to me.

I found more motivations to take part in technology:

Living in two different capitalist economies, I find how cruel we are to our resources and environment. I believe the thought that we don't need to be like this has always been in the back of my mind all this time.

A big part of childhood was made up by the Internet. Growing up in the digital age, technology seemed to become second nature, yet its flaws become more and more apparent as I grow up. Issues of ethics, privacy, sustainability, they all pop up one after another.

As I ponder about my path, I always think back to my origins, but lately, I've also been thinking about my destination.

When I was in fourth grade, the concept of mortality, my inevitable ending, pushed me into a state in need of attention and comfort.

A few days ago, I recall of it again after reading a story of a character's death. But this time, I thought about how it will be as I approach my end. I started thinking about what I wanted in life and what responsibility I wished to uphold. One of those things was doing good for the world. I wanted to help the planet and the people who gave me ground to step on and words to speak of.

So, I wish to combine my origins and desired future through technology related to Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning. I know that these are buzzwords, and in all honesty, I do not know enough to explain these concepts well enough, but this unknown also drives me to discover their possibilities. I want to create technology that can save and improve lives and allocate Earth's resources efficiently.

I make sure to stay clear on priorities and my vision despite hesitation. I set up a CS advisor meeting to talk about potential for graduate school and research. I am taking classes on Linear Algebra, requirement classes, and classes in other fields such as philosophy and psychology. I am planning taking EECS 445 (Machine Learning), EECS 442 (Computer Vision), and either EECS 492 (Artificial Intelligence) or just do self-studying in AI topics. I also improve myself through other resources, such as learning how to communicate.

As I do all of this, patience keeps me present.

**thought #2**

I always have a similar experience when I end a novel. It's as if I don't want to leave their emotions, their world, their story. I come to terms with being alone and often enjoy isolation, but it's a different feeling recognizing other people's journey in loneliness and fulfillment. Maybe it's because we are all individual beings. Even though I do have doubts on what makes us individuals and if collective existence exists, it's most straightforward for me to think of myself as one and another as another.

The nature of being an observer can bring about loneliness. All we can do is watch the story begin, unfold, reveal, and settle. Sometimes I wonder what tugs the strings in my hearts for these fictional people, the emotional music that I listen while I read, the selfish empathy I hold towards them, my imagination that uniquely colorizes its nuances and details? Maybe those factors do play some kind of role, but I hope just the story between them and their definition of love and companionship shape my emotions the most.

In a society that values happiness and happy endings, I find myself attached to this one story with a sad ending for too long. We often define a relationship and a story with its ending, rather than its in-between, and perhaps this is human nature, to be the one who survives and wins at the end. So how must we defy out human nature, and do we even need to? As I write this part, I seem to be coming into closure and acceptance with sad endings by recognizing the preciousness of the memories not of the last moment, but of the vivid memories of every moment.

Love, I think, exists when it starts to exist. We cannot destroy it. Even if the ones who produce such love no longer exist, love will persist with those remaining.

I think we love happy endings because we tend to think happy endings mean happy forever. But time is limited, and we will receive an inevitable ending. It may be better to think that we each have many stories in life that have their "happy endings" or "bad endings". Either way, we just have to be live the present, as we carry the love in our hearts.
