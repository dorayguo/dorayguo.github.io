**12/27/2020: Reading review on "What if you could do it all over?" by Joshua Rothman**

I read [this article](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2020/12/21/what-if-you-could-do-it-all-over) from the New Yorker that discussed how we often imagine alternative lives that we could have led, analyzed different perspectives to such behavior, and argued how what we could have become shapes who we are. 

The author Joshua Rothman reiterated an interesting idea more than once: "Our self-portraits use a lot of negative space." The last time I encountered the use of negative space before today was in my university's dance studio, where we learned to be aware of the space around us. It is like a reminder that the space around us isn't empty and can outline the emotions, shape, and location of someone. A trick into real life is to notice what is not there to grasp what is there, specially contemplate why is one action or decision not taken to investigate why one was taken.

A outstanding line by Jean-Paul Sartre was quoted, "A man commits himself and draws his own portrait, outside of which there is nothing. No doubt this thought may seem harsh.... But on the other hand, it helps people to understand that reality alone counts, and that dreams, expectations, and hopes only serve to define a man as a broken dream, aborted hopes, and futile expectations." For a second, the negative descriptions behind such "dreams, expectations, and hopes" shook my idealistic mindset, but on the other hand, what better motivation is there for us to act than to be real.

Likewise, mentioning the hesitation of the decision to change ladders (or paths) in the middle of climbing injected some reality into me, as it revealed the struggles of the inescapable linearity of our lives and the difficulty of mustering courage as we carry out responsibilities and identify risks. This realization is more about decision-making, but the author's previous illustrations of the juxtaposition between one character pursuing a destructive but passionate life and the other for a boring and automative one in Tolstoy's Anna Karenina and the countless "what-if" scenarios that a young man thought about after comparing himself to his successful peers that choose a different decision than him in Henry Jame's The Jolly Corner. It is from history that we reap lessons for our way, and I often find fictional stories to bring clarity to my mind regarding possibilities in life I should consider. I don't wish to dwell on the past for so long, stopping my footsteps in the present and clouding my vision for the future. I don't wish to going about my daily chores in life without knowing where I aim to land; I can enjoy having fun at the moment but not continuously move aimlessly. In connection with previously-mentioned negative space, I should have clear reasons, compiled with regard to my thought-through priorities, behind why I take a certain decision and why I don't take others. And those priorities should be constantly evaluated with my identiy, principles, and vision and against the time/environment and people aroudn me. I will most likely need more real life experience to create specific examples, but my main takeaway for my current state is to think clearly about my goals and to understand clearly the reasons behind my actions and behaviors.

Although maybe a little stretch, the life course that we take is almost like walking down a tree that has an infinite amount of children decisions that are intertwined with dependence and variability. The journalist also mentions constraints in life, which made me connect to the branch-and-bound algorithm. William Empson stated that "[there] is more in the child than any man has been able to keep", and I can't say whether that is good or bad other than that I hope to protect the child me and grow the mature me.

Oh and a quick tip I may take is to keep a diary (or in this case, writings of some sort) to eliminate any mismatch I feel between my objective and subjective evaluation of myself, that may fuel, if otherwise not closened, my perceived deepness and the vibrancy of our lives.

**12/27/20: Lecture review of "How to Speak" by Patrick Winston**

I vaguely remember of watching [this lecture on Youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Unzc731iCUY) a few years ago, but looking at how I currently speak now, it will be beneficial for a second review of content and corresponding practice, as Professor Winston outlines these main 2 factors in the quality of communicatio. He reflects the different components in speaking well in his lecture speech. And I will outline them as following. One thing to note is that these techniques seem to suit public speaking the most but overlap and transfer to conversational speech quite well.

The professor starts off emphasizing the importance of attention. We only have one language processor, and the audience can only understand the message if they receive it in the first place. He mentions some ways of grabbing attention later on, but not much in detail, such as when speaking as a minority, which is understandable.

The speaker shouldn't start off with a joke, but rather with a promise: what the listener will know after the speaker speaks.

Some speaking strategies:
- To reinforce an idea, cycle on it.
- To not confuse the audience a certain idea with others, build a fence around it (what it is and isn't)
- To recenter audience attention, use verbal punctuation like providing an agenda of the material and enumerating/numbering.
- To engage attention, ask questions (can't be too obvious or hard) + wait 7 seconds.
- To inspire, show passion

Logistics:
- Time: lecture at 11am because people are awake and not sleepy from eating
- Location: good lighting (awake people), cased (check the place out for familiarity and practice as if there were people), and reasonably-populated

Objects:
- Chalkboard for informating (for good speed of comprehension, graphics, and pointing to the board)
- Props are interesting and attention-grabbing
- Slides for exposing (less slides, less words, less clutter, 40/50pt min font)
- Speaker and slides should be close to each other and less need to switch attention
- Face & engage the audience 

Applications:
- Presenting research: situate context of the problem and practice with people unfamiliar with your research
- Job: use 5 minutes to present your vision and what you've done to reach that vision
- Fame: receive recognition for our work by giving it a symbol, a slogan, a surprise, salience (one idea that sticks out), and a story

Lastly, to wrap a talk up, you can tell a joke, provide contributions and time to read them, or salute the audience with appreciation (but saying thank you is not a strong move).

For next time, I will prepare a speech with an apparent backbone, always find ways to grab attention to and make clear of easily-absorbable material, and practice effectively. Another advice to note is to learn from our preferred speakers and build our personal style.

**12/28/20: Documentary review of "Better brain health" by DW Documentary**

"You are what you eat." [This documentary](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=&esrc=s&source=web&cd=&cad=rja&uact=8&ved=2ahUKEwiX5ZHKsojuAhU7GVkFHc57D4gQwqsBMAB6BAgBEAM&url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.youtube.com%2Fwatch%3Fv%3DTLpbfOJ4bJU&usg=AOvVaw3ctRWHOWNN_27MVz7sYEk6) backs up this common saying with convincing and attention-deserving research. One study examines memory decline in rats who are deficient in Omega-3s. Another study looks at cannabilistic hamsters from a deficienty in Vitamin B3. Data following more than 20,000 women giving birth found more aggresiveness associated with a more unhealthy diet during pregnancy. A study with people show tendency to choose an action that compromises self-interest in face of feelings of unfairness that is correlated with eating a higher sugar-to-protein breakfast. Another study with flies show a small group who do not eat more protein as consequence of a protein deficiency, associated with difference in gut bacteria, suggesting the importance of eating a diverse set of foods for maintaining a diversity of gut bacteria. Diets closer to the Mediterranean diet is proven to be more beneficial in reducing depresseion. The video also emphasized the viscious and causal cycle between eating sugars and wanting sugars from receiving related cues.

This documentary strongly persuades me to stop eating junk food, eat diverse foods of important nutrients, and be aware of what goes into my body and the consequences.

**12/28/20: Documentary review of "AlphaGo" by DeepMind**

The news of AlphaGo winning world professional Lee Sedol came out around four years ago, but at that time, I wasn't particularly interested in it as much as I am now, perhaps from my deepening interest in and knowledge (though not much at my current stage) of artificial intelligence.

In a game like Go that reflects ourself to the board, AlphaGo's victory makes me wonder: will humans eventually become not unique compared to self-learning artificial intelligence? Will it show that what makes humans human is not really unique? Will we reach beyond our human limits using the tools we create? And will we even understand what they can reach? Will our future society operate on artificial intelligence? Will we normalize the 'black box' and the unknowns behind the actions and behaviors of its AI? How do we regulate them and ensure ethical and moral reasoning and constraints when AI is self-learning? On which principles should they be focused on: gaining as much territory as possible or just enough to win like with AlphaGo? How do we respond to weaknesses and mistakes that it makes?

These are only the few of many questions I have for artificial intelligence. I haven't dived Rdeep enough into the theoretical or practical knowledges of AI, but it will be interesting to look back on these inquiries I have now with the skillset and perspective I will have then. Perhaps these questions are valid or invalid, answered or awaiting a discussion, important or not.

Watching [this documentary](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WXuK6gekU1Y) and thinking about these questions push me towards the importance of philosophy. Right now, my understanding on what is right, what is good, who we are, what we should do, and many more concepts are mostly based on my opinion and 19-year old experiences. I would like to gain more perspective and insights from history.
